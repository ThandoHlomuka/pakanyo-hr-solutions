Why L&D Must Be a Strategic Driver, Not Admin

June 2026 · By Bella Ngwenya

Most organisations don't have a training problem. They have a capability problem. And too often, L&D is expected to fix it with workshops.

Across organisations, learning is still treated as an event. People attend training. Registers are signed. Certificates are issued. Reports are submitted. Then everyone goes back to work — and very little changes.

I've worked with organisations where learners complete skills programmes but still struggle to apply what they've learned. I've seen managers attend training and return to the same behaviours. I've supported teams where compliance targets were met, yet capability gaps quietly continued to grow.

Eventually, the business feels it. Productivity drops. Engagement declines. Errors increase. Good people leave.

I don't see learning and development as a support function. I see it as a business capability.

The Real Issue: L&D as Administration

Too often, L&D is positioned as administrative. Focused on coordination. Focused on compliance. Focused on delivery. Rarely positioned as a driver of organisational performance.

But learning should not exist to tick boxes. It should exist to build capability.

Real development happens when programmes are designed around actual performance challenges. When behaviour change matters more than attendance. When managers are actively involved. When learning continues after the training room. When accountability exists.

When learning is disconnected from strategy, it doesn't just fail learners. It becomes a business risk. Because organisations don't succeed in training hours. They succeed in what people can actually do.

If L&D remains administrative, organisations will keep recycling the same problems — the same capability gaps, the same disengagement, the same leadership struggles, the same turnover.

Are you delivering training — or are you building capability?

Leadership: Where the Gap Shows Most

Leadership transitions are one visible example of a much wider issue. A strong technical performer gets promoted and suddenly becomes responsible for people, performance, conflict, emotions, and decision-making — without preparation for that shift.

I've walked into organisations where first-time managers were overwhelmed, avoiding difficult conversations, unsure how to manage performance, and struggling to motivate their teams.

Not because they lacked potential. Because development was never designed around real workplace demands.

When L&D Becomes Strategic

But when L&D becomes strategic, everything shifts. Learning supports performance. Managers are developed intentionally. Teams function better. Risk is reduced. People stay.

This isn't about adding more programmes. It's about building capability on purpose.

That means L&D must:

  • Understand business priorities, not just training requests
  • Design learning that supports real workplace demands
  • Measure behaviour change, not participation
  • Embed learning into daily work
  • Hold leaders accountable for development outcomes

L&D cannot remain administrative. Not in today's environment. If learning is not shaping capability, culture, and performance, then we are simply moving people through programmes — not developing organisations.

It's time for L&D professionals to take their place as strategic drivers. Not as support. Not as coordination. But as business partners responsible for building the capability organisations depend on.
